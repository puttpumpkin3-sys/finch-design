01 · Thread entry cards P1 · Ship first

A typed feed, not chat bubbles.

The current implementation defaults to chat-bubble styling for everything. The thread is not a chat — it's a typed log of artefacts (transcript, analysis, your notes, supervisor replies, briefings, command receipts) that happen to be threaded by day. Each entry type has its own visual shape, and the bubble style is reserved only for back-and-forth supervisor exchanges.

02 · Cancelled & rescheduled P2 · Daily

Visible, but quiet.

Coaches reschedule a lot. The card needs to register the change without judgment, keep awareness ("I knew that was the one I lost"), and fade visually so it doesn't compete with the live sessions for attention. Default behavior: dashed strikethrough. Don't hide cancelled cards — coaches lose track when sessions silently disappear.

Decisions
Cancelled → strike-through with CANCELLED pill, dashed border, 60% opacity. Rescheduled → amber pill, old time crossed out, new time below. Empty day (all cancelled) → warm prompt card, not a hero panel.
Awareness over noise. The card's information stays — only its visual weight changes. The reflection prompt steps in when the day clears, not when one session moves.

03 · Non-coaching events P2 · Reduces noise

A footer log, not a banner.

When the AI pre-screen detects a calendar event isn't a coaching session — a team standup, a sales call — the coach should know it was skipped, and have a one-click way to override if the AI was wrong. Default treatment: a quiet "Other today" footer at the bottom of Home, not a toast or banner. The point is to reduce noise, not introduce a new noisy surface.

Decisions
Skipped events → quiet log at the bottom of Home. Override → inline button on the row, no separate review screen. Footnote count → integrated into the row header, not a hover.
Coaches glance at Home all day. A toast is intrusive; a settings screen is forgotten. A bottom log is exactly the right priority — visible if you scroll, invisible if you don't need it.

3.3 · Optional one-time toast (alternate)

For coaches who want a louder signal

Settings → Notifications · per-coach preference
Notify me when an event is auto-skipped
A toast appears for ~6 seconds when Coach Claude decides a calendar event isn't coaching. Off by default — most coaches use the bottom-of-Home log instead.
Annotation · why log over toast (default)
We considered three options: toast, banner, log. Toast interrupts; coaches told us the dashboard is meant to feel calm. Banner persists and adds visual weight. The bottom-of-page log is the editorial choice — present if needed, absent in attention. The toast is exposed as a per-coach preference in Settings → Notifications for coaches who want stronger signals.
04 · Sparse home P3 · Polish

When the day is quieter.

The 3-up grid was specced for the modal day — three or more sessions. With one session, two sessions, or none, the same grid looks like a card with empty parking lots beside it. The grid responds to its content. Fewer cards means more breathing room, more typographic detail, and a different surrounding context.

05 · Your Library P3 · New surface

Reference materials, folded into supervision.

Books, frameworks, articles the coach wants Claude to draw on. Lives as a fourth tab in My Practice — Approach · Learning · Library · Report — because it sits alongside CoachContext and Learning as part of the coach's living philosophy. Not Settings (it's not a config), not its own route (it's not a destination). The trust signal is critical: coaches need to see how these materials feed the supervision, or it feels like an abandoned upload form.

Decisions
Placement → 4th tab in My Practice. Layout → upload zone above, three-column grid of journal-cards below, grouped by type. Trust signal → persistent strip showing "Claude references these when reviewing your sessions".

06 · Dictation mode P4 · Nice-to-have

Speak it, don't type it.

Coaches told us they'd rather speak their philosophy than type it. The "How I Coach" section accepts a recording, transcribes it, and lets the coach edit. This should feel like dictating into a journal — quiet, private, no corporate "transcription complete" chrome. Used in onboarding (screen 2) and Settings (CoachContext editor) — same component, same feel, two entry points.

How the room works for you

When you walk into a session, what's the first thing you're listening for? What's the room you try to make?

Transcribed from 2m 14s of audio · April 23
The first thing I'm listening for is whether the client is in their head or in their body. Most senior leaders walk in cocked — they've already structured what they want to talk about, and that structure is usually a defense. So my job, in the first ten minutes, is to not let them lead with it. I'll ask a body question — "where are you right now, before we start" — and the room I try to make is one where it's safe to not have an answer.

Annotation · error states use the amber strip, NOT a red banner
The notebook vocabulary doesn't have a destructive-error pattern by design — coaching is a forgiving surface. Both errors above use the .nb-reflect amber-strip pattern, just with different lab icons (alert-circle for permission, alert-triangle for service failure). Heading uses Newsreader italic — same as a reflection prompt. Body copy is plain Inter, addressing the coach directly with a concrete next step, not a stack trace. Buttons keep the strip's amber/cream color — never switch to red. The "Type instead" escape hatch is always present; it's the kindest fallback.
Annotation · error copy rules
Permission denied: tell the coach where to fix it (address bar lock icon), not just what happened. Most coaches don't know browser permission UIs. Transcription failed: distinguish "we got the audio but the service didn't reply" from "your mic gave us nothing" — different fixes. Save audio locally on the client (IndexedDB) so a retry doesn't ask for re-recording. Auto-retry once silently on transient network errors before showing this strip.

Annotation · why default differs
In onboarding, the coach hasn't typed yet — Speak is the inviting path, Type is the "I'd rather not" fallback. In Settings, the coach is editing existing text — Type is the natural mode, Speak is the "let me re-do this section out loud" affordance. Same component, different default. Persists per-coach in User.uiPrefs.dictationDefault.
